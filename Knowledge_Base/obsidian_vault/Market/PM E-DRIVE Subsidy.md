# PM E-DRIVE Subsidy Scheme

The PM E-DRIVE (Electric Vehicle Revolution in Vehicle Enhancement) scheme is India's third-generation E2W subsidy program, succeeding FAME-II (Apr 2019-Mar 2024) and the bridge scheme EMPS-2024 (Apr-Sep 2024). Effective October 2024 through July 31, 2026, it provides Rs 5,000 per E2W via an e-voucher mechanism — an 87.5% reduction from FAME-II's maximum of Rs 40,000 per vehicle. The scheme has a total outlay of Rs 10,900 Cr across all vehicle categories (E2W, E3W, E4W, e-buses), is fund-limited (meaning it could exhaust before the terminal date if demand surges), and uniquely requires minimum 50% Domestic Value Addition (DVA) for eligibility.

## The DVA Advantage

The 50% DVA requirement is the most strategically significant feature of PM E-DRIVE for Ola Electric. Unlike FAME-II, which had no domestic sourcing mandates, PM E-DRIVE explicitly requires that at least 50% of the vehicle's value be sourced domestically. This creates a structural advantage for Ola, which manufactures cells in-house at its Krishnagiri Gigafactory and produces vehicles at its Futurefactory. Import-dependent competitors — including Ather (which sources cells from international suppliers) and legacy OEMs transitioning their supply chains — face either a DVA compliance burden or exclusion from the subsidy.

The DVA requirement aligns with Ola's vertical integration strategy and provides a policy-driven moat that partially compensates for Ola's service-related competitive disadvantages. However, this advantage is temporary: the scheme expires in 2.5 months, and competition may restructure supply chains to meet DVA requirements in a successor scheme, if one is announced.

## The July 2026 Cliff

The expiration of PM E-DRIVE creates a clear demand shock risk. The historical precedent is instructive: when FAME-II expired in March 2024 (subsidy dropped from Rs 40,000 to the Rs 5,000 EMPS-2024 bridge), the total E2W market dropped 29% MoM from 119,320 to 84,463 units, and Ola dropped 36% from 53,320 to 33,963 units. The Rs 5,000 loss from PM E-DRIVE's expiry is smaller in magnitude than the Rs 35,000 drop from FAME-II to EMPS-2024. However, the cumulative effect of two years of subsidy erosion (Rs 40,000 to Rs 5,000 to Rs 0) may produce greater price sensitivity fatigue among consumers, especially when combined with state-level policy withdrawals.

At Ola's current EBITDA margin of +4.5% (Q4 FY26), the per-unit profit is approximately Rs 4,050. Removing Rs 5,000 in subsidy would entirely eliminate this profit if passed through to consumers. Ola faces three options: (1) absorb the Rs 5,000 and return to near-zero margins, (2) pass the cost to consumers and risk demand contraction, or (3) achieve further BOM reduction through Gigafactory scale to offset the loss. Option 3 is the most viable long-term but requires volume recovery to spread fixed costs.

## State-Level Divergence

The post-July 2026 landscape will be defined by state-level policy divergence. With central subsidies gone, the competitive advantage shifts to states that maintain their own EV incentives. Tamil Nadu (Ola's home state) continues 100% road tax exemption plus Rs 10,000-20,000 per E2W subsidy through Dec 2027. Maharashtra offers Rs 10,000/E2W through 2030. Delhi offers up to Rs 30,000 (Year 1 tapering). This creates geographic winners and losers — Ola benefits from Tamil Nadu's continued support, while competitors with sales concentrated in Karnataka (which ended 100% road tax exemption in Apr 2026) face a compounded disadvantage.
