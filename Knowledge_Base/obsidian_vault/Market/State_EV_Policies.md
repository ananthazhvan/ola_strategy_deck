# State EV Policies

India's federal structure means EV policy operates at two tiers simultaneously: central schemes (FAME-II, PM E-DRIVE, ACC PLI) set the national framework, while individual states layer their own incentives — subsidies, road tax exemptions, registration fee waivers, and toll concessions — creating a patchwork of demand conditions that directly impacts Ola's unit economics and market access strategy. The divergence across states is material: a customer in Delhi can receive up to Rs 30,000 in state subsidy (6x the current central PM E-DRIVE amount), while a customer in Gujarat receives zero state subsidy since its discontinuation in 2025.

For Ola Electric, the state policy landscape has three strategic implications. First, Ola's manufacturing base in Tamil Nadu gives it home-state advantages that partially compensate for competitive disadvantages elsewhere. Second, the Karnataka road tax reversal (Apr 2026) creates a headwind in what should be Ola's strongest urban market — Bangalore accounts for a disproportionate share of India's EV adoption. Third, the combination of central subsidy expiry (Jul 2026) and state-level withdrawals (Gujarat) or reversals (Karnataka) creates a compounding demand risk that is only partially offset by supportive states (Tamil Nadu, Maharashtra, Delhi).

The net effect is that Ola's geographic revenue mix will increasingly determine its profitability. In states with supportive policies, Ola can maintain competitive pricing; in states without them, either margins compress or volumes decline. This creates an additional layer of strategic complexity that competitors with diversified ICE revenue streams can absorb more easily.

## Tamil Nadu

Tamil Nadu's EV Policy (2019 through Dec 2027) provides the strongest state-level support to Ola Electric, offering Rs 10,000-20,000 per E2W subsidy plus 100% road tax and registration fee exemption. Ola's Krishnagiri-based Futurefactory and Gigafactory are located in Tamil Nadu, qualifying the company as a home-state manufacturer under the policy. This is strategically significant for two reasons.

First, the Tamil Nadu subsidy effectively doubles the central PM E-DRIVE subsidy (Rs 5,000) for Ola customers in the state, creating a total incentive of Rs 15,000-25,000 per vehicle — approaching FAME-II levels. Second, the SGST reimbursement program for manufacturing provides direct cash support to Ola's factory operations, boosting other income. Ola's "Other Income" grew from Rs 98 Cr (FY21) to Rs 391 Cr (FY25) partly from these state incentives.

The policy extends through Dec 2027, meaning Tamil Nadu will continue providing support for 17 months after the central PM E-DRIVE cliff — a crucial buffer that aligns with Ola's Gigafactory Phase 2 timeline (12 GWh by Jul 2027) and full build-out (20 GWh by Mar 2028). This makes Tamil Nadu-registered customers Ola's most attractive demographic from a policy perspective.

## Karnataka

Karnataka's road tax policy change (Apr 2026) is the most significant state-level headwind facing Ola Electric. The state ended its 100% road tax exemption for EVs and replaced it with a tiered Lifetime Tax (LTT) of 5-10%, increasing on-road prices by approximately Rs 7,000-14,000 on a Rs 140,000 scooter. This is strategically damaging because Bangalore is one of India's most important EV markets — high disposable income, early adoption culture, and strong charging infrastructure make it a must-win geography for any EV player.

The Karnataka change is especially poorly timed for Ola. The company's fragile recovery was showing stabilization at ~16% market share and ~39K quarterly units by Q4 FY26. The tax increase creates a fresh demand headwind that could disrupt this recovery trajectory. For Ola's most popular model — the S1 X 3kWh at Rs 89,999 — the tax increase of Rs 4,500-9,000 represents 5-10% of the vehicle price, a meaningful burden in a price-sensitive segment.

Ola's headquarters are in Bangalore, and the company has a concentrated customer base in Karnataka. The state was Ola's natural home market. The road tax reversal means Ola now faces a net policy disadvantage in its home state while benefiting from policy support in Tamil Nadu (where its factories are but fewer customers live) — a geographic mismatch that complicates Ola's recovery strategy.

## Gujarat

Gujarat discontinued its direct EV subsidy in 2025, retaining only the 1% motor vehicle tax waiver and registration fee exemption. This removes a significant demand incentive in one of India's most prosperous and EV-friendly markets. Gujarat had previously offered competitive subsidies and was an important state for E2W adoption, with high disposable income and strong urban-rural connectivity making it a natural EV market.

The discontinuation is part of a broader trend of state-level EV subsidy rationalization as governments reassess fiscal priorities. For Ola, Gujarat's withdrawal matters because it removes demand support in a high-volume state, compounding the pressure from the central PM E-DRIVE cliff. Competitors with lower cost structures or ICE cross-subsidy capability (Bajaj, TVS, Hero) can absorb the loss of subsidy more easily than Ola, which relies on every rupee of policy support to maintain competitive pricing while its margins remain thin.

## Delhi

Delhi has the most aggressive EV policy in India, with a draft 2026 policy offering up to Rs 30,000 per E2W in Year 1 (tapering structure) plus 100% road tax waiver for EVs under Rs 30 lakh. This is 6x the central PM E-DRIVE subsidy of Rs 5,000. For Ola's S1 X 2kWh at Rs 69,999, the Delhi subsidy represents a 43% price advantage — potentially making Ola scooters dramatically cheaper in Delhi than in states without such subsidies.

Delhi's policy is designed to combat the capital's severe air pollution, giving it political sustainability that other states may lack. However, the tapering structure (subsidy reduces over time) creates a temporary window of opportunity. For Ola, Delhi is a critical battleground market — it is a high-volume, high-visibility urban market where younger, tech-forward consumers (Ola's target demographic) are concentrated. The combination of aggressive state subsidy and Ola's low entry price point (Rs 69,999 for S1 X 2kWh before subsidy) could make Delhi Ola's strongest market if the service recovery narrative takes hold.

However, the Delhi policy also benefits all EV players equally. Ather, TVS, Bajaj, and Hero customers also receive the same benefits. Ola does not have an exclusivity advantage here — it competes on product and trust, where it remains weakest.

## Maharashtra

Maharashtra's EV Policy (2025-2030) offers Rs 10,000 per E2W with 100% road tax and registration exemption plus toll waivers — making it one of the most comprehensive state EV policies in India. The Rs 10,000 subsidy is double the central PM E-DRIVE amount, creating a total incentive of Rs 15,000/E2W for Maharashtra customers. The policy runs through 2030, providing long-term demand visibility that most other states lack.

Maharashtra's importance to Ola is strategic. Mumbai and Pune are among India's top EV-adopting cities, with the state's high-income urban population being a natural fit for electric two-wheelers. The toll waivers are a unique feature that provides ongoing cost savings for daily commuters, adding to the total cost of ownership advantage. For Ola, Maharashtra represents an opportunity to regain premium-market share — the state's customers can afford higher-ASP models (S1 Pro, S1 Pro+) where Ola's margins are better.

Ola's service crisis hit hardest in urban markets where complaint visibility was highest. Maharashtra's urban concentration means trust recovery here is both more difficult and more important. If Ola can demonstrate service improvement in Mumbai and Pune, the policy tailwind + restored trust could make Maharashtra a strong recovery market.

## Key Data Points

- Tamil Nadu: Rs 10,000-20,000/E2W + 100% road tax exemption + SGST reimbursement for manufacturers (through Dec 2027)
- Karnataka: Ended 100% road tax exemption Apr 2026; replaced with 5-10% tiered LTT = Rs 7,000-14,000 price increase
- Gujarat: Direct EV subsidy discontinued in 2025; only 1% motor vehicle tax waiver remains
- Delhi: Up to Rs 30,000/E2W in Year 1 (draft 2026 policy) — 6x central subsidy — plus 100% road tax waiver
- Maharashtra: Rs 10,000/E2W + 100% road tax + registration exemption + toll waivers (through 2030)
- Central PM E-DRIVE cliff Jul 2026: Rs 5,000 per E2W lost — state policies become the only subsidy layer post-cliff
- GST on EVs: 5% vs 28%+cess on ICE — 23+ percentage point price advantage

- Ola's Krishnagiri factory qualifies for Tamil Nadu home-state manufacturer benefits
- Ola's Bangalore HQ faces Karnataka road tax headwind — geographic policy mismatch
